How this world is made
  • Three noise fields. Elevation, moisture and temperature are independent layers of smooth random (simplex) noise — the same trick Minecraft uses. Flip to the Height / Moisture / Heat views to see the raw layers.
  • Thresholds. The sea & mountain sliders are simple cut-offs on elevation. Everything in between is classified by (temperature, moisture) into a biome — a Whittaker diagram lookup.
  • Rivers start on high ground and flow downhill until they reach the sea (or pond up). They moisten nearby land, so valleys turn green.
  • Towns are placed by scoring cells (flat, near water, mild climate) and keeping the best ones that are far apart. Roads are A* paths that avoid steep slopes and pay a toll to bridge rivers.

How wave function collapse works
  • Every cell starts as a superposition of all 8 tiles (dim = uncertain).
  • Each step, the algorithm picks the cell with the lowest entropy (fewest / most lopsided options left) and collapses it to a single tile, chosen randomly using the weights above.
  • Constraints then propagate: a tile may only touch itself or its immediate neighbours in the chain deep → water → sand → grass → forest → hills → mountain → snow. Impossible options get deleted, which can cascade far across the map.
  • Paint a tile to force a choice and watch the ripple. Painting something the constraints forbid (snow in a lake, say) melts the neighbourhood back into uncertainty, and it heals around your choice.
